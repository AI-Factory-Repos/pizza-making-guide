The Dough

Great pizza starts with great dough. Expand each step below for detailed instructions and video demonstrations.

📏 Pro Tips

  • Always weigh ingredients — volume measurements are inconsistent for baking.
  • Water temperature matters: cold water slows fermentation; warm water speeds it up.
  • Add salt after the flour and water are combined — direct contact with yeast can inhibit activity.
  • 00 flour creates a softer, more extensible dough ideal for Neapolitan-style; bread flour gives NY-style its chew.

How you mix the dough affects gluten development, texture, and final structure.

1

Autolyse (Optional but Recommended)

Combine flour and water — hold back the salt and yeast. Mix until just combined (no dry flour). Rest 20–30 minutes. This hydrates the flour and kick-starts gluten development with minimal effort, making kneading easier.

2

Add Yeast

Dissolve yeast in a small amount of the recipe water (lukewarm, 100–110°F / 38–43°C) for active dry yeast. Add to the dough and mix until evenly incorporated.

3

Add Salt & Oil

Sprinkle salt over the dough. Add olive oil if using. Incorporate by folding and pressing — the dough will feel slippery at first, then come back together.

4

Mix to Shaggy Dough

Mix until no dry bits remain and a rough, shaggy ball forms. At this stage the dough does not need to be smooth — that comes from kneading.

By Hand

Use a bowl scraper and bench scraper to bring the dough together. Fold the edges into the center and press down, rotating the bowl as you go.

Stand Mixer

Use the dough hook. Mix on low (speed 2) for 2 minutes to combine, then medium (speed 4) for 6–8 minutes until the dough clears the bowl sides.

Food Processor

Pulse dry ingredients, then add liquid with the machine running. Process 30–40 seconds until a smooth ball forms. Dough will be warm — refrigerate before proofing.

Kneading develops gluten, creating the elastic network that traps CO₂ bubbles and gives pizza its signature chew and structure.

1

Traditional Push-and-Fold

Push the dough away with the heel of your palm, fold it back toward you, rotate 90°, and repeat. Work for 8–10 minutes until smooth and elastic. The dough should spring back slowly when poked.

2

Slap & Fold (Rubaud Method)

Pick up the dough, slap it against the bench, fold the top third down and the bottom third up (like a letter). Rotate 90° and repeat. This is excellent for high-hydration doughs (65%+). Work for 5–7 minutes.

3

Stretch & Fold (No-Knead)

Leave the dough in the bowl. Every 30 minutes, wet your hand, grab one edge, stretch it up as high as possible, then fold it over the top. Rotate the bowl 90° and repeat 4 times per set. Do 4–6 sets over 2 hours.

⚠️ Kneading Checklist

  • Avoid adding extra flour — a sticky dough is normal. Use a light mist of water or a scraper instead.
  • Work on an unfloured surface for push-and-fold to maintain friction.
  • The dough is ready when it passes the windowpane test (see video below).
  • Over-kneaded dough is tight and tears rather than stretches — if the dough tears frequently, rest it 10 minutes.

Fermentation is where flavour develops. Longer, cooler proofs produce more complex, tangy flavour. Shorter, warmer proofs are more neutral but still delicious.

1

Bulk Fermentation

After kneading, shape the dough into a smooth ball. Place in a lightly oiled bowl, cover with plastic wrap or a damp towel. The dough should roughly double in size. Look for volume, not just time.

2

Divide & Ball

Turn the dough onto an unfloured surface. Divide into equal portions (typically 250–300 g each for a 12" pizza). Shape each portion into a tight ball by folding the edges under and rolling on the surface to create surface tension.

3

Second Proof (Ball Proof)

Place dough balls in a lightly oiled container or tray, spaced apart. Cover tightly. Proof at room temperature for 1–2 hours, or refrigerate for up to 72 hours. Remove from fridge at least 2 hours before baking to come to room temperature.

✅ How to Tell if Dough is Properly Proofed

  • Poke test: Press a floured finger ½" into the dough. If it springs back slowly and partially, it's ready. If it springs back immediately, it needs more time. If it doesn't spring back at all, it's over-proofed.
  • The dough should feel airy, pillowy, and slightly jiggly when the container is shaken.
  • Over-proofed dough is still usable — bake it promptly and handle it gently.

Even experienced bakers run into issues. Here are the most common dough problems and how to fix them.

Dough is too sticky and won't come together

Cause: Excess hydration, undermixing, or warm environment.

Fix: Use a bench scraper and wet hands rather than adding more flour. Work the dough through stretch-and-fold sets. Refrigerate for 20 minutes to firm it up, then continue.

Dough tears when stretched

Cause: Underdeveloped or over-stressed gluten (tight dough).

Fix: Cover and rest the dough for 15–20 minutes to allow the gluten to relax, then try again. Do not force it.

Dough isn't rising

Cause: Dead or inactive yeast, too-cold environment, or salt killed the yeast.

Fix: Test yeast by dissolving in 110°F water with a pinch of sugar — it should foam in 10 minutes. Place dough in a warm spot (75–80°F) or inside an oven with just the light on.

Dough is over-proofed (too puffy, collapses)

Cause: Left to ferment too long or in too-warm an environment.

Fix: Gently degas the dough by pressing it down, reshape into balls, and bake immediately. The flavour may be more sour but the pizza will still be edible. Reduce yeast or proof time next time.

Crust is too dense / no air bubbles

Cause: Under-proofed dough or insufficient oven temperature.

Fix: Allow more bulk fermentation time. Bake at the highest oven temperature possible (500°F+). Preheat your baking stone or steel for at least 45–60 minutes.

Crust is too tough / chewy

Cause: Over-kneaded dough, too much bread flour, or insufficient water.

Fix: Increase hydration by 2–3%. Try blending 50% bread flour + 50% all-purpose flour. Avoid over-working the dough after balling.

Dough springs back and won't stretch

Cause: Dough is too cold or gluten is too tense.

Fix: Let the dough warm up to room temperature (at least 60–90 minutes out of the fridge). Between attempts, cover and rest 10 minutes. Work from the center outward, never pulling from the edges.
