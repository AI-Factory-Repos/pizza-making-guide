Sauce Preparation

A great pizza starts with a great sauce. Explore the three classic bases below, learn when to cook your sauce versus keeping it fresh, and master the seasoning and consistency techniques that separate good pizza from unforgettable pizza.

🍅 Basic Marinara

The cornerstone of Neapolitan pizza. A true marinara is surprisingly simple — its quality depends entirely on excellent tomatoes and restraint with seasoning.

Core Ingredients

  • San Marzano or high-quality crushed tomatoes (400 g / 14 oz)
  • Extra-virgin olive oil (2 tbsp)
  • Garlic (2–3 cloves, thinly sliced)
  • Fresh basil leaves (a generous handful)
  • Fine sea salt (to taste)
  • Optional: pinch of red pepper flakes, dried oregano

Step-by-Step Method

  1. Gently warm olive oil in a saucepan over medium-low heat.
  2. Add garlic slices; sauté 1–2 min until fragrant — do not brown.
  3. Add crushed tomatoes; stir to combine.
  4. Simmer uncovered on low heat for 20–25 min, stirring occasionally.
  5. Remove from heat, tear in fresh basil, and season with salt.
  6. For a smoother texture, blend briefly with an immersion blender.

🧂 Seasoning Techniques

  • Salt in stages: season lightly at the start and again at the end after tasting.
  • Acidity balance: if the sauce tastes too acidic, add a small pinch of sugar or a splash of olive oil.
  • Umami boost: a tiny amount of anchovy paste (½ tsp) dissolved during cooking deepens savory notes.
  • Herb timing: dried herbs (oregano) go in early; fresh basil goes in off the heat to preserve brightness.

📏 Consistency Tips

  • Too thin: simmer uncovered longer to evaporate excess moisture.
  • Too thick: add a splash of water or tomato juice and stir over low heat.
  • Ideal spread consistency: the sauce should coat the back of a spoon but still flow slowly.
  • Drain canned tomatoes slightly if they are very watery before cooking.

🥛 White Sauce (Béchamel / Alfredo)

Creamy white sauces offer a rich, indulgent base perfect for white pizzas, chicken toppings, or vegetables. Choose between a classic béchamel or a simpler Alfredo-style approach.

Béchamel Ingredients

  • Unsalted butter (2 tbsp / 30 g)
  • All-purpose flour (2 tbsp / 20 g)
  • Whole milk (1 cup / 240 ml), warm
  • Salt, white pepper, and freshly grated nutmeg
  • Optional: grated Parmesan (30 g) for extra richness

Alfredo-Style Ingredients

  • Heavy cream (½ cup / 120 ml)
  • Unsalted butter (2 tbsp)
  • Garlic (1 clove, minced)
  • Parmesan, finely grated (50 g)
  • Salt and white pepper to taste

🧂 Seasoning Techniques

  • Nutmeg: a tiny pinch (⅛ tsp) is the classic finishing touch for béchamel — don't skip it.
  • White pepper instead of black keeps the sauce visually clean and adds subtle heat.
  • Garlic powder (¼ tsp) stirred in off the heat adds aroma without bitterness.
  • Cheese integration: add cheese after removing from heat to prevent graininess.

📏 Consistency Tips

  • Roux ratio: equal weights of butter and flour ensure a smooth base — whisk constantly while cooking the roux for 1 min.
  • Add milk gradually in three additions, whisking between each to prevent lumps.
  • Too thick: whisk in warm milk a tablespoon at a time.
  • Too thin: return to low heat and stir until desired thickness; avoid high heat which can scorch.
  • Pizza application: white sauce should be slightly thicker than marinara to prevent a soggy crust.

🌿 Pesto Variations

Pesto brings vibrant, herby freshness to pizza. The classic Genovese basil pesto is just the beginning — explore sun-dried tomato and arugula variations for exciting flavour profiles.

General Pesto Tips

  • Always toast nuts lightly before blending — it deepens flavour significantly.
  • Blanch basil leaves for 5 seconds then shock in ice water to keep the sauce vivid green.
  • Use a food processor for speed or a mortar and pestle for traditional texture.
  • Add olive oil gradually while blending for a smooth, emulsified result.
  • Pesto can brown on pizza if baked at very high heat — apply after baking or in the last 2 minutes.

Cooking vs Fresh — Pesto

  • Fresh (no-cook): Pesto is almost always used raw, applied after the pizza exits the oven to preserve its colour and fresh taste.
  • Lightly baked: dollop pesto on halfway through baking for a slightly roasted, mellowed flavour.
  • Never fully bake from the start — the heat destroys the fresh herb notes and turns the sauce brown.
  • Storage: cover pesto with a thin layer of olive oil in an airtight container to prevent oxidation; keeps 5 days refrigerated.

🧂 Seasoning Techniques

  • Salt balance: Parmesan and pecorino are already salty — taste before adding any extra salt.
  • Acidity: a squeeze of fresh lemon juice brightens all pesto varieties.
  • Garlic intensity: use raw garlic for punch or roasted garlic for a sweeter, milder profile.
  • Cheese ratio: a 2:1 Parmesan to Pecorino blend gives the best balance of sharpness and creaminess.

📏 Consistency Tips

  • Too thick: blend in extra olive oil or 1–2 tbsp of pasta water (or plain water) a little at a time.
  • Too thin: add more nuts or cheese and re-blend.
  • Pizza spread thickness: pesto should be spread thinner than tomato sauce — it is rich and concentrated.
  • Texture preference: pulse less for a chunky, rustic pesto; blend longer for a silky smooth result.
